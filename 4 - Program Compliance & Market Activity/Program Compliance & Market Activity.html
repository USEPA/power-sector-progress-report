Overview

The Acid Rain Program (ARP) aims to curtail acid-rain-causing emissions from the electric power sector through an emissions trading program. The suite of Cross-State Air Pollution Rules (CSAPR, CSAPR Update, Revised CSAPR Update, and Good Neighbor Plan (GNP)) are designed to address cross-state air pollution, which affects downwind states’ ability to attain and maintain air quality standards. To learn more about these programs and their history, visit the Progress Report Program Basics Page. For additional information on how emissions trading programs work visit EPA’s  Emissions Trading website.

Compliance for ARP and each CSAPR trading program is assessed on an annual basis, while market activity (i.e., allowance trading) is monitored throughout the year. Each regulated facility must hold allowances equal to or greater than its emissions for the relevant compliance period by each program’s allowance transfer deadline. If a facility fails to hold sufficient allowances by the relevant deadline, it is subject to additional allowance surrenders. In addition, “assurance provisions” are included in the CSAPR programs to help ensure that each covered state achieves the required emissions reductions (Visit EPA's CSAPR Assurance Provisions for more information). Historically, all of the power sector allowance trading programs have had exceptionally high rates of compliance. This trend continued in 2023 as demonstrated in the table below.

Market Activity

Market-based programs encourage participants to find cost-effective ways to comply with the emissions limit (or cap) set by EPA. Companies accomplish this by finding the most efficient and economically viable emission reductions not only among their own generating assets, but across the entire marketplace of power generators. Market participants may buy, trade, sell, or bank allowances for use in future years. In addition to ensuring compliance, EPA tracks allowance holdings and records transactions to better understand market trends.
